🧠 Why the kitchen feels different now

This is useful to know — not scary, just helpful. Skip ahead any time.
Why does the kitchen feel different now?

After something frightening, your brain puts itself on alert. It starts watching for anything that reminds it of that moment — a smell, a sound, a feeling of heat, even the light in the room. When it spots something, it sends an alarm. Your heart races, your body tenses. It feels like it's happening again.

Your brain is doing what it's supposed to do — keeping you safe. The trouble is, it can't always tell the difference between then and now. So it sounds the alarm even when everything is actually fine. That's a brain doing its job too well.

A trigger is anything that reminds your brain of the moment you got hurt. A smell. A sound. Heat. Your brain is trying to protect you — but sometimes it's too sensitive. The good news: once you know what your triggers are, they have less power over you.

Common triggers after a kitchen injury:
🔥 Sight of a flame
🔊 Sizzling or popping
👃 Smoke or burning smell
♨️ Steam or heat on skin
🍳 The feel of a pan handle
💡 Bright overhead light
When a trigger hits — what helps:
  • Name it: "That's a trigger. My brain is protecting me."
  • Use a calming tool (🌿 Pause button — always there)
  • Say: "That was then. This is now. I am safe."
  • Step away if you need to — that's always okay

🧤 Your kitchen safety kit

Before you start climbing, here are the tools that make every step safer. Smart cooks use all of these.
🧤 Long oven mitts — up to the elbow

Regular pot holders only protect your hands. Long mitts protect your forearms — the area most at risk from splatter and steam. Put them on before you touch anything hot. Take your time. No rush. If you have scarring or limited grip, ask your care team about adaptive equipment that works for you.

⏱️ Kitchen timers — for attention, not just cooking

Here's something every cook does: you mean to be right back, then life happens — a phone call, a child, a thought — and more time passes than you realized. A timer means the kitchen reminds you it needs attention. Use it every single time. Even experienced cooks do.

🍳 Lids, not water — for grease fires

If a grease fire starts in a pan, slide a lid over it carefully — that smothers it. Never water on grease. Keep a lid near the stove before you start. This one piece of knowledge changes everything.

🌬️ Air fryer or oven first

Air fryers and ovens use enclosed heat — no open flame, no splatter. For foods you used to fry, an air fryer gives great results with much less risk. Starting here is a smart way to return. Some people use their air fryer forever. That's a great outcome too.

📞 Ask someone to stand by

For your first few attempts, ask someone to be available — even just by phone or text. Not to watch you. Not to help. Just to know you're trying, and to be reachable. This is good strategy.

Who is your standby person? (just for you — not stored anywhere)
👨‍👩‍👧 When others are in the kitchen with you

Children: Give older kids a job — keeping younger ones occupied, setting the table, something in another room. You don't have to manage the kitchen and the children at the same time.

What you know now: You know things about kitchen safety that most people don't. Teaching others — your kids, your friends, your neighbors — what you've learned is one of the most powerful things a survivor can do. And it helps you heal too.

⚠️ A note before heat steps

If you have recent grafts or areas where your skin is still healing, please check with your care team before trying any heat-related steps. Heat sensitivity varies a lot depending on where you are in recovery. There is no rush.

📖 What hard things can show us

Studies show that many people who go through serious injuries find unexpected things about themselves on the other side — patience they didn't know they had, a clearer sense of what matters, or a quiet strength that wasn't visible before.

This doesn't mean the injury was okay, or that you have to find a silver lining. It just means that getting through hard things often leaves something behind — and sometimes that something is worth noticing.
